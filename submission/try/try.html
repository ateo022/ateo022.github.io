Art of the Web

Portfolio by Adele

Readings

Week 2

The central question of the piece, "What is Mail Art"—and by extension "What is Art"—forced me to consider the elements that constitute it. When thinking about a piece of art, both medium and support are typically considered essential to creating the piece. However, changing the support (e.g., the postage system vs relying upon friends) does not remove the artist's intention of making his creation. Thinking about the mail artist's intention of choosing his support, I realized that the most jarring difference between mail art, from art as we mentally conceptualize it, is in its size and complexity, leading me to wonder: is the enigmatic "Big Monster" Carrión refers to in his writing meant to be taken literally? I.e., "Mail Art knocks at the door of the castle where the Big Monster lives" conveys that mail art is a direct and intentional opposition to the notion that art should be Big and grand as we often see in museums. Further, can a work that is not big (or categorized under the "Fine Arts") then be considered art?

Week 3

The idea that a website could be incomplete was initially hard for me to conceive as before reading this piece, my concept of a website aligned with what users typically see on Instagram's, Google's, etc. interfaces. However as Schwulst implores readers to create their own spaces on the web, I began to understand the web in terms of its meaning to people who use it rather than the financial purposes behind social media platforms. As people consume information, much of it becomes integrated into their understanding of the world, and hence a part of one's identity. Then is a website, a reflection of one's specially crafted space on the web and a part of their identity, able to fully reflect reality? Does the dimensionality or format of the web limit us in self-expression?

Week 4

As a user, I find that the death of web pages is something slightly hard to think about. It is a pity that so many website creators put in a great deal of effort into their work, but the continual evolution of the web have let projects be put on halt or lost. It is also interesting how the reading concluded that age has no value, as the development of a website is not linear. Given that our projects could be involuntarily shut down with the constant changes on the web, does the uncertainty for preservation of web-related projects increase the value of it?

Week 6

After reading the text, I realized that alt-text is intrinsically useful for individuals who do not interact with the web in typical ways. It allows accessibility of web functions across different needs. Moreover, it can be seen as self-expression by the author as he/she has the freedom to write the alt-text how they see fit. I had a similar question to the one raised in the reading on whether alt-text should be written objectively or subjectively. Ultimately I feel that navigating websites itself is a subjective process, as they are often a result of curated flows by their designer. Hence, introducing subjectivity into alt-text may end up enriching the experience of browsing a website for those who need it.

Week 8

After finishing this reading, it was interesting to realize that code is often language-specific. More likely than not, the programming language is built around English, rendering non-speakers unable to learn this skill. As the author points out, it is difficult to adapt alternative versions of the coding language to suit a non-English user due to flaws in the premise of programming. However, it is still necessary for us to create opportunities and spaces for such sections of society in the digital space. The author proposes, at the end of his paper, to create a new inclusive language from scratch, which makes me wonder if such a feat is possible at all. How can we merge different culture-specific linguistic patterns of languages to unify programming? What sort of losses in nuances will arise from such a move?

Week 9

Duvall explores the Internet as a series of carefully crafted hyperlinks. In a day and age where we are highly reliant on the Internet, hyperlinks are a quintessential part of our online experience. Hyperlinks can be used as a creative tool to disrupt linear narratives and encourage audience participation. How, then, can we leverage this existing network to bring about novel experiences on our sites? Further, must we necessarily use hyperlinks in our design, given how crowded/cognitively demanding the online space is today.

Week 11

Frenkel's innovative approach to exploring absence through multimedia art is intriguing. The paper effectively highlights how Frenkel challenges traditional media boundaries and invites deeper thoughts on the theme of loss. However, I wonder how Frenkel's personal experiences or cultural background may have influenced her artistic exploration of absence. Additionally, I'm curious about the reception of "String Games" among different audiences and how interpretations of absence may vary across cultural contexts.

Week 12

This week's reading discusses the flow of information and how it has evolved. More specifically, as the direct recipients of these forms of information, we all the more should have a vested interest in what we consume. As technology becomes more advanced, we run the risk of having our feed flooded by unwanted digital traffic. While Soulellis implores readers to resist this paradigm by actively archiving, interrupting, printing, etc. their feed, what would be the result of no one heeding his advice? Would the digital world look much different from how it is now—have we already entered a state of heightened desensitization to technologies such as predictive videos?

Week 13

This week's reading was straightforward and explicit in its purpose of encouraging readers to tap into their existing relationships to form communities. These valuable support systems that thrive on human trust and care for one another are directly opposed to the controlling nature of softwares. Ultimately, the author intended to convey that the hierarchy in which we subject ourselves to when using technology is opposed to our human needs and thus unnatural. My only question then, would be whether deep connections that ultimately give rise to a sense of belonging and community, are still valid if they were built through online platforms.

Week 14

This week's reading surrounded the use of the Internet as an archive for artists where Groys highlights the Internet's role in challenging traditional notions of authorship, ownership, and distribution in art. He argues that the internet challenges traditional notions of authorship by enabling collaborative and participatory forms of creation. Additionally, Groys discusses how digital art complicates issues of ownership and authenticity, raising questions about the commodification of art in the digital age. Overall, his nuanced exploration highlights both the opportunities and challenges presented by the internet for the creation, dissemination, and reception of art. This leads me to my question in response to his mention of the copy and paste: while the action encourages participatory behavior and lowers the barriers to being involved in art, when should we draw the line between art and users' thoughtless use of such a simplified means of disseminating art? How can creators prevent such abuse of their works online?
